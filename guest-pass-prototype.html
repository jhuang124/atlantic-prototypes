The Crumbling Foundation of America's Military

The U.S. failed to produce weapons and ammunition fast enough to supply Ukraine. Could it equip its own armed forces in the event of war?

By Mark Bowden
Red Capitol building with architectural detail [illustration]
Mar 18, 2026
Ukraine Defense Weekly
4:23 12:45

Toward the end of 2023, as the war in Ukraine entered its second year, American military strategists found themselves confronting a sobering realization: the United States was not sufficiently prepared to fight a sustained conflict with a near-peer competitor. At the heart of this concern lay a question that cut to the very foundations of American military power: Could the nation produce enough weapons and ammunition to supply both Ukraine and, if necessary, defend itself?

The answer, documented in military assessments and congressional testimony, was unsettling. American defense contractors, hamstrung by decades of budget constraints and peacetime procurement patterns, simply could not generate the volume of munitions required for extended operations.

This shortfall has profound implications not just for Ukraine, but for America's ability to project power and defend its interests globally. The crisis exposed critical vulnerabilities in the defense industrial base—a network of thousands of suppliers, manufacturers, and facilities spread across the country.

What makes this situation particularly grave is that it arrives at a moment when American military dominance can no longer be taken for granted. The rise of China, the continued ambitions of Russia, and the spread of advanced weaponry to nonstate actors have created a strategic environment far more complex than the one that prevailed for much of the post-Cold War period.

For decades, the assumption underlying American defense policy was simple: the United States could draw on its economic and technological superiority to outmanufacture any rival. But that assumption is being tested in ways not seen since the early years of the Cold War.
