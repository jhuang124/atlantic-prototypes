The President's Phone Has Become a National‑Security Threat

Trump's casual approach to digital communications leaves the country vulnerable.

By Barton Gellman
Alex Brandon / AP
March 11, 2026, 7 AM ET

The Supreme Court handed the first blow to Steve Bannon, a pivotal figure in the right-wing establishment who fell from power only to stage what he called a "restoration" before going off to prison.

For three years, Trump's most ideologically fervent advisers have been working to consolidate presidential power at a pace without modern precedent. They replaced career officials with loyalists, invoked emergency authorities of dubious legality, and challenged the independence of agencies that had operated with relative autonomy for decades.

What made the phone a particular vulnerability was its intersection with several of the administration's weakest points: the blurring of official and personal communication, the president's well-documented reluctance to follow security protocols, and the aggressive foreign intelligence collection that U.S. officials say has intensified since January.

National-security lawyers in both parties have warned that the current arrangement creates what one former official described as "a standing invitation to every intelligence service on the planet." The physical security of the device is only part of the problem — metadata, location data, and communications patterns all represent collection opportunities even without accessing content.

Nothing about the legal framework has been equipped to handle a president who treats encrypted messaging apps as interchangeable with secure government channels, or who shares information across platforms with no regard for classification boundaries. The result is a gap between what the law permits and what operational security requires — and adversaries, by all accounts, are exploiting it.

Perhaps the most revealing moment came during a briefing in the Roosevelt Room, where officials presented the president with evidence of attempted foreign access to his personal communications. According to three people present, the president's response was to ask whether the briefing itself had leaked — not to ask about the security implications of the breach.
